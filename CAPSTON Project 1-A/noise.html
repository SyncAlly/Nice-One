Noise Pollution

Noise pollution is unwanted or disturbing sound that disrupts the natural balance of life. It can come from many sources and has serious effects on human health, wildlife behavior, and overall quality of life.

Decibel Levels

Whisper: 30 dB

Conversation: 60 dB

Traffic: 85 dB

Jet Engine: 140 dB

Prolonged exposure to sounds above 85 dB can cause hearing damage

Common Sources

  • Road traffic and vehicle horns
  • Aircraft and airports
  • Industrial machinery and factories
  • Construction equipment and activities
  • Loud music and entertainment venues
  • Household appliances and equipment
  • Railway and subway systems

Health Effects on Humans

  • Hearing loss and tinnitus (ringing in ears)
  • Sleep disturbance and insomnia
  • Increased stress and anxiety levels
  • High blood pressure and heart disease
  • Reduced concentration and productivity
  • Headaches and fatigue
  • Irritability and aggression

Impact on Wildlife

Animals rely on sound for communication, navigation, and survival. Noise pollution disrupts these essential behaviors.

Marine Life: Sonar and ship engines interfere with whale and dolphin communication.

Birds: Traffic noise forces birds to sing louder or at different times.

Land Animals: Noise from development drives animals away from their habitats.

Reducing Noise Pollution

Individual Actions

  • Lower volume on devices
  • Use noise-canceling headphones
  • Install soundproofing materials
  • Maintain vehicle mufflers
  • Avoid honking unnecessarily

Community Solutions

  • Plant trees and vegetation barriers
  • Enforce noise regulations
  • Create quiet zones near schools
  • Use quieter machinery
  • Design better urban planning

Important Notice

The World Health Organization recommends that average noise levels should not exceed 55 decibels during the day and 40 decibels at night to prevent health issues.
